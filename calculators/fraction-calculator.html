    How fraction operations work

    addition: a/b + c/d = (a×d + c×b) / (b×d)
    subtraction: a/b − c/d = (a×d − c×b) / (b×d)
    multiplication: a/b × c/d = (a×c) / (b×d)
    division: a/b ÷ c/d = (a×d) / (b×c)

    After the operation, the result is simplified by dividing the numerator and denominator by their greatest common divisor (GCD), so you always get the fraction in lowest terms.

    A fraction's denominator tells you the size of each piece. You can only add or subtract pieces that are the same size, so both fractions are rewritten over a shared denominator (here, the product of the two denominators) before combining the numerators.

    Multiplying fractions just multiplies numerators together and denominators together — there's no need for matching piece sizes. Dividing by a fraction is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal (flip the second fraction), so it follows the same rule.
